01 What is meMO?

A shared model for the safety argument.

meMO is a way to describe medical-device safety evidence as a connected engineering model instead of a pile of separate documents. It gives names and structure to the things teams already reason about: intended use, hazards, harms, risk controls, requirements, architecture, behavior, verification, evidence, and release baselines.

The useful part is that those relationships become checkable. A team can ask whether a high-risk control is allocated to real behavior, whether it has acceptance criteria and evidence, or what must be re-reviewed after an interface, workflow, configuration, or software change. meMO does not replace engineering judgment. It makes the thread of that judgment visible, reviewable, and easier to keep current.

02 The problem

Traced across silos, but not meaningful.

Requirements, risk files, architecture notes, tests, DHF records, reviews, and releases often change on different cadences. The issue is not a lack of documents. It is missing, checkable meaning between them. Linked does not mean complete.

Medical has the standards, not the model
StandardsISO 14971, IEC 62304, ISO 13485, and ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010 define expectations
ArtifactsRMF, V&V matrix, DHF, architecture, tests, reviews, and releases hold the evidence
Architectureinterfaces, modes, timing, allocation, and behavior show where safety is realized
Missing layera low-cost model that ties standards, process, and design together
The review question: what changed, and what must be re-reviewed?

03 Architecture at the core

Put the safety argument where change actually happens.

meMO operationalizes a low-cost, architecture-centered model where the whole chain is typed and checkable: intended use to hazards, controls, requirements, architecture, behavior, verification, evidence, and release baseline.

Methodology

Rigor profiles, viewpoints, rules, gates, workflow stages, and project bindings that guide how the ontology is applied.

Parser, semantic graph, rule engine, CLI workflows, import/export, DHF generation, and CI validation.

Architect
